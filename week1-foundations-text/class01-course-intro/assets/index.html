LLM Capabilities vs Job Automation: Pareto Analysis

How mid-2026 frontier and open-weight language models stack up against the automation potential of real occupations. The Pareto frontier highlights jobs with the highest automation potential relative to what today's models can actually do.

Updated July 2026 · Model landscape as of mid-2026 · Occupation framing based on the Anthropic Economic Index.

Key Insights from the Anthropic Economic Index (2026)

  • Augmentation is back on top (52% vs 45%): after automation briefly led in mid-2025, collaborative "thinking-partner" use again dominates automated hand-offs on Claude.ai.
  • The agentic shift: long-running tasks via Claude Code and Cowork now rival chat; 54% of those coding sessions run on the most capable models.
  • Workers expect a rising tide: over 1 in 3 survey respondents expect AI to do most or nearly all of their work tasks within 12 months.
  • Coding still leads: computer & mathematical tasks make up ~30% of heavy Claude usage — far above their ~4% share of US employment.
  • Delegators are optimistic: heavier automation users report the most positive expectations for pay, job security, and skill value (57% say AI makes their skills more valuable).

How to Read This Chart

Jobs (circles): automation potential vs how well current LLMs match the required capabilities. Color = displacement risk profile.

High Risk — routine knowledge work (data entry, translation, basic copy)
Medium Risk — analytical & support roles (paralegals, analysts, junior devs)
Augmentation — expert roles AI assists but rarely replaces (engineers, physicians, lawyers)
Low Risk — physical / hands-on / high-touch work (nurses, electricians, chefs)

Models (diamonds):

Methodology & Data Sources

Occupation framing draws on Anthropic's Economic Index, which maps millions of Claude conversations onto O*NET/BLS occupational tasks to estimate observed exposure (tasks already done with AI) versus theoretical exposure (tasks an LLM could plausibly do). The "Current Usage vs Potential" view illustrates the gap between what AI is already used for and its estimated ceiling per occupation cluster.

Model positioning: frontier and open-weight models are placed using mid-2026 capability signals (Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index, SWE-bench / GPQA-style benchmarks, and public leaderboards). The top of the field is extremely tight — Claude Fable 5, Claude Opus 4.8, Gemini 3.1 Pro, GPT-5.5 and Grok 4.3 sit within a few points of one another, while open-weight models (DeepSeek, Qwen3, Kimi K2, Llama 4) now reach roughly 85–95% of frontier capability at a fraction of the cost.
